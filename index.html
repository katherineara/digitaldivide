in the United States and California

CPSC 490 Senior Project - Part 2
Katherine Arackaparambil
Advisor: Holly Rushmeier
ABSTRACT
Through the use of the Javascript Library D3.js, HTML, and CSS, this project visualizes data about access to the internet and computers in order to illustrate the Digital Divide in the United States and California. The Digital Divide is defined as the unequal access of technology to different groups based on social, demographic, or geographic factors. Due to the global pandemic, technology has become an essential part of work, communication, education, and more. Even though technology seems ubiquitous in the 21st century, many people still lack the tools to succeed in this increasingly digital environment. With new social distancing recommendations, most schools have had to transition to online learning and many offices have closed for employees. Students and families are more reliant on technology now than ever before, yet this privilege is not afforded to everyone equally. Even in California, where there is a focus on technology and innovation in the Silicon Valley, not everyone has access to these basic resources. Black, Latino, rural, and low-income households in California have been disproportionately affected, which further deepens the divide between these communities. This project utilizes census and survey data to create an interactive visualization that illustrates the disparities in access to technology and ways to close the gap.
BACKGROUND
This project further explores the work started by Amanda Lee on Visualizing the Digital Divide in New York. Instead of New York, this project will focus on the United States and Calfornia after the impact of the pandemic. To create this project I used D3 which is a Javascript library for data visualization. I used the skills I learned from the course Data and Information Visualization with Professor Rushmeier and Professor Brown. I hosted the HTML, CSS, and JS code on a Heroku-Server and I used VS-Code with Airbnb Style Formatting.
GRAPH 1
In the first graph, I use bar graphs to portray the percent of students in households with no internet or computer access by income from 2016 and 2020. In the second graph, I use bar graphs to illustrate the same percentage based on race. The data was collected from USA Facts and the Census Data from the American Community Survey.

Design Choices
Since we have the categorical variables of income and race on the x axis and the quantitative variable of percentage on the y axis, I decided to use bar graphs to represent the data in a clear and concise format. By using double bar charts grouped together, the differences between computer and internet data can be compared more easily. I also allow the user to interact with the data by having an option to view the results in alphabetical, ascending, or descending order. Hovering over the graph allows the user to see the percentages. Clicking on the button transforms the bar charts using a smooth transition. This can help the user to easily see which groups are the most and the least affected by the digital divide.





Takeaways
From analyzing the first graph, it is clear that lower-income households have a major disadvantage in getting access to computers and the internet. The percentage of people who lack internet or computer access consistently declines with more wealth. The graph also shows that on average, there are more people who have access to the internet than computers. Some people might have internet connection in their homes in order to use their other devices like smartphones, but computers are very expensive which might explain why some people have more difficulty accessing them than others.

The second graph shows that groups of people who are American Indian, Black, and Hispanic have a harder time on average getting access to the computer and internet. However, people from an caucasian or asian have a lower percentage of people who lack access to these basic needs. It is interesting to compare the data from 2016 and 2020 to see if there have been any improvements in the Digital Divide. There have been many initiatives and programs to close the gap over the last few years, however after looking at the data, it appears that the same inequalities are still present.
GRAPH 2
In the second graph, I use data from the Household Pulse Survey to look at the percentage of people that have access to a device available for educational purposes. I plotted various different categories based on age, gender, education, race, marital status, children, employment status, food sufficiency, and household income.

Design Choices
I used a line chart because I wanted to show the trends over time starting from April to November 2020. The Household Pulse Survey from the US Census Bureau was a new program that was designed to collect information about the impact of the global pandemic on people’s lives in the United States. The user can click on the different categories to see the results from that data presented on a line chart and the trend over time in 2020. By clicking on the different categories, the user can look at how this data changes based on the different social and demographic factors. The colors differentiate the lines and are labeled in the legend to provide more insight into the trends. By hovering over the data points, the user can see the percentages I calculated using the raw data. I took the 9 different months from 2020 and went through each spreadsheet, calculating the percentages for each category and adding it to a csv file. It was a time-consuming process to clean, aggregate, and calculate the data from multiple sources, but it was very informative once the dataset was finally visualized.


Takeaways
These line graphs provide important insights into which groups have more or less access to computer devices for education based on multiple factors. For these graphs, a higher data point is better because that means a larger percentage of people have access. The age graph illustrates that people who are older have more access compared to a younger demographic. Even though children and youth are the ones who need devices for educational purposes the most, not all of them have it for connecting to their online courses.In the gender graph, there is also a slight gap between male and female ownership of devices, which reflects the gender gap in many areas of life. In the educational graph, those who have a higher educational degree like college and bachelor degree have more access to technology than students in elementary or high schools. The graphs by household income and race emphasize the same findings from the previous visualizations. The food sufficiency graph reveals that some households who don’t have enough food at home may also have a lack of connectivity to the online world. In some of the graphs there appears to be a dip in the percentages around the months of June and July. There could be multiple reasons for the drop in technological access like unemployment, but that cannot be inferred from this dataset. But overall there appears to be an upward trend on most of the graphs, which hopefully means that the divide is improving and more people are finding resources to access the internet and computers.
GRAPH 3
This is a choropleth map that divides up California into different counties to see how geographic location and other factors can have an impact on technology access. The centroids in each county are shown by the circles. The saturation of the colors show the broadband subscription rate in each county with the darker color indicating a higher percentage. The size of the circles correlates to the median household income. Hovering over the county also shows the covid cases in that county per 1M people on a bar graph.

Design Choices
I used a json of the California counties and the geoAlbersUSA projection to plot the path points onto the map. Then I used data from the California Gov Open Data Portal to get more information about the average household income and then combined that with census data about the percentage of broadband subscribers in each county. I decided to use a choropleth map because I wanted to see if there were any geographic areas that had less access to the internet and if location has an impact on technology accessibility. I also wanted to compare the broadband subscription rate to the covid cases to see if there was any correlation between them.




Takeaways
It appears that the less dense and rural areas have a lighter blue color on the map which indicates that they have a lower percentage of households with broadband subscriptions. The cities like the San Francisco Bay Area also have both the largest centroid circles and darkest blue color which indicates that they have a higher median household income and more broadband subscriptions. There is such a large focus on supplying resources to large cities, but that often leaves others behind who also need it. There doesn’t appear to be a direct correlation between coronavirus cases and access to broadband services, because there are many other factors that might affect this statistic more directly.
GRAPH 4
In the final map, I wanted to show how cities are taking initiatives to bridge the digital divide and to see if they were heading in the right direction. Since we know that the internet is needed in areas with low income, I used a choropleth map of the City of San Francisco divided up into its different neighborhoods. SFGov has set up multiple free Wifi hotspots in the City in an effort to give people more access if they need it. These parks and recreation centers are plotted on the map.

Design Choices
I decided to use a map because that helps with visualizing the neighborhood better than a bar chart or line chart. I used a json of San Francisco and the geoAlbersUSA projection to build the paths and map them with D3. Then I combined that information with household income data and unemployment data from the American Community Survey census study that analyzed each neighborhood in SF. The darker blue color correlates with the average household income in that neighborhood. I researched the various places that SF was offering free Wifi hotspots and I calculated their latitude and longitude points to place them on the map. I added interaction so that they could hover over places on the map and see the names of the location.





Takeaways
With the Silicon Valley located in California, the West Coast is known for being a hub for technology and innovation. Especially in San Francisco, there are many initiatives to focus on Computer Science education and give people the tools and skills to access technology and increase digital literacy. However, there are still disparities between different groups within the community and a need for more laptops and digital devices at home now that it is more difficult to share between families when everyone needs access to strong internet. When schools across the state went online in Spring 2020, thousands of laptops and hotspots were needed for students to continue their education. It looks like the City is giving people Wifi Hotspots but they can still do more to spread their reach to the different neighborhoods and other parts of the city that need it. By setting up more resources to reach people in groups that have less access to the internet and computers, the government and organizations can help bridge the Digital Divide.
CONCLUSION
The global pandemic has brought to light many racial and socioeconomic disparities that have divided our country even further apart. Many people who might have basic necessities like food and shelter don’t have access to the internet or tech devices, which are becoming an increasingly essential part of living in the digital age. Without the internet, it becomes challenging to work, learn, grow, and connect with others in a safe way that doesn’t put lives at risk for the virus. This project allowed me to visualize the digital divide and illustrate that there are still many more steps, initiatives, and programs that can be put in place to support those who need access to technology, so we can move forward and keep innovating, creating, and learning - without leaving anyone behind.
